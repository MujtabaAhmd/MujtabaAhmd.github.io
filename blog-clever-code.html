The Hidden Cost of Clever Code

On the seductive pull of abstraction, and why the most maintainable systems tend to be the most boring ones.

There is a particular kind of pride that comes from writing code nobody else can immediately understand. A chain of nested ternaries. A one-liner that does the work of ten. A recursion so elegant it practically hums. I have felt this pride. I have also, later, felt its consequences.

The Trap

Clever code is not a crime. Sometimes the clever solution really is the right one — tighter, faster, less surface area for bugs. But cleverness has a cost that doesn't show up in the moment of writing. It shows up at 11pm six months later when something breaks and you are the one who has to fix it.

"Debugging is twice as hard as writing the code in the first place. Therefore, if you write the code as cleverly as possible, you are, by definition, not smart enough to debug it." — Brian Kernighan

I used to think this was a quote about other people. Less experienced developers who hadn't yet learned to read complex abstractions. It took me an embarrassingly long time to realise it was a quote about everyone, including me, including you.

What Boring Looks Like

The most maintainable codebases I've worked in share a quality that is hard to articulate until you've seen its opposite: they are unsurprising. You open a file and the code does what the filename suggests. Functions do one thing. Variable names are verbose to the point of redundancy. There is almost no cleverness anywhere.

This is not an accident. It is the result of developers who have been burned before, who have inherited their own past cleverness and paid the price, and who now write code primarily for the reader rather than the author.

A Rule I Try to Follow

Before committing anything that gives me that quiet satisfaction of having been clever, I ask: could someone who joined this team last week understand this without asking me? If the answer is no, I rewrite it. Not because I doubt their intelligence — but because I won't always be there to explain it.

Cleverness is a debt. The interest compounds silently, and it always comes due.
