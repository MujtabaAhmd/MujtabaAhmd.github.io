My Decision is Correct, Is It?

You made a decision and thought it was rational and purely logical. Think again. Most of our decisions are based on our emotions and the environment.

You have an "inner animal" — a term coined by Robert Greene. He suggests that we are not entirely rational and are just higher order animals whose judgement is clouded by emotions.

"We like to think of our decisions as being based on facts and logic, but they are almost always based on our emotions, which are then justified by logic." — Robert Greene

We tend to justify our decisions and hide from the consequences whether it be positive or negative. We see a person lash out and become angry at others and after some time confronting the person, they usually justify their stance. This is quite natural.

This influences our daily lives, our decisions, our actions. Our biases are extremely strong, and we fail to recognize them. The specific term used in psychology is motivated reasoning. Without realizing this or any kind of awareness, this bias cannot be overcome. Your emotions will always cloud your decision and judgement.

Two Methods to Counter It

There are 2 methods that can be adopted to avoid this: Falsification and Cognitive Reframing.

In falsification, you ask: how can I be wrong? This forces you to find the negative aspects of your position to "debunk" your own proposal. In mathematics, we falsify a conjecture — a statement believed to be true but not yet proven — by finding a single case where it fails. 1,000,000 successful cases don't "prove" a theorem; one single counterexample kills it forever. This is the same logic in falsification: you find out where your argument and hypothesis need revaluation.

Cognitive reframing works in conjunction with this. You reframe how you look at the situation, shifting your perspective. There are multiple frames to draw on:

  • Devil's advocacy frame — Shift the perspective to the negative points of the situation. It is the practical application of falsification.
  • Curiosity over correctness frame — Reframe the problem as a learning experience instead of a win/loss situation. Your ego will never accept a loss, but your curiosity has nothing to protect.
  • Objective observer — How would a third person who has no stake in this situation interpret it?
  • Temporal reframing — After 5 years, how would I look at this situation?

These frames will prove helpful in your journey, but there are more to be discovered. You just have to understand which tool to reach for in your current situation.
